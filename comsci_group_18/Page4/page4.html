Hip Hop

From graffiti walls to global charts

What is hip hop all about?

Hip hop is the heartbeat of the streets. Raw energy, bold expression, and unstoppable rhythm. Born from block parties and booming bass, it blends slick beats, sharp lyrics, and fearless creativity into a culture that moves the world. It’s more than music, it’s style, story, swagger, and soul. If you want sound that speaks truth and makes you move, hip hop is where the fire lives.

Pre 1990s History

Hip hop sparks from the soul of the Bronx in the early 1970s, where young Black and Latino communities transformed limited resources into limitless creativity. DJs like Kool Herc pioneered the “break”—isolating the funkiest parts of a record—while MCs energized crowds with rhythmic chants that would later evolve into rap. Block parties became the birthplace of a new cultural language, blending music, dance, and community in a way the world had never seen.

As the late ’70s and ’80s arrived, hip hop rapidly expanded in sound and ambition. Innovators like Grandmaster Flash and Afrika Bambaataa shaped DJing into an art form, while acts like Run-D.M.C., LL Cool J, and Salt-N-Pepa pushed rap into mainstream consciousness. Breakdancing crews turned cardboard into battlegrounds, and graffiti writers turned subway cars into rolling galleries—proving hip hop wasn’t just a genre but a full-blown cultural movement.

By the time the 1990s neared, hip hop had grown from local block parties to a national force. Its messages—raw, real, and rebellious—resonated far beyond New York. Regional scenes bubbled up, artists experimented with new flows and sounds, and the world began to recognize hip hop as a powerful medium for storytelling and social commentary. The foundation was set for the genre’s explosive rise in the decades to follow.
